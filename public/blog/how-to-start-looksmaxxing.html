How to Start Looksmaxxing: A Beginner's Guide

Published 14 June 2026. A grounded primer.

Most people who ask how to start looksmaxxing meet the noise before they meet the answer.

Forums full of jargon. Photographs with no context. A hundred techniques, each promising a different face by next month. Most of it is distraction. The truth is quieter, and more encouraging: the things that change how you are seen are mostly things you already control. This is the calm version. What looksmaxxing actually means, what to do first, what to leave alone, and how long it really takes.

What looksmaxxing actually is

Looksmaxxing is the practice of deliberately improving your physical presence. Not chasing someone else's face. Maximising your own. It covers your skin, your hair, your grooming, your body, the way you carry yourself, and the way you dress. Stripped of the theatre, it is an old idea wearing a new word: take care of yourself, on purpose, with attention.

The useful part of the word is "maxxing." It points at a ceiling that is yours, not a comparison to anyone else. Your task is to move toward the upper edge of your own range. That is a reachable goal. Becoming a different person is not, and pretending otherwise is where most beginners lose their footing in the first week.

Softmaxxing and hardmaxxing: start soft

You will meet two terms quickly. Softmaxxing is everything non-surgical: skincare, grooming, fitness, posture, style, sleep, the daily habits that compound. Hardmaxxing means medical or surgical intervention, from orthodontics to cosmetic procedures.

The order matters more than the labels. Almost everyone underestimates how far softmaxxing alone will carry them, because they have never run the fundamentals consistently for long enough to find out. Surgery is irreversible, costly, and easy to regret when it is reached for early, out of impatience rather than need. So begin soft. Give the basics a real, honest season. If, after that, something specific still troubles you, you can weigh more with a clear head and a qualified professional. Softmaxxing first is not timidity. It is sequence.

The fundamentals, in order

Here is where to actually begin. Five areas. None of them exotic, and that is the point.

  • Skincare. The simplest routine that works: a gentle cleanser, a moisturiser, and sunscreen every morning. Sun protection is the most evidence-backed thing you can do for how your skin ages, so it earns its place before anything fancier. Add nothing else until that is automatic. For a specific concern, a dermatologist is worth more than any forum thread.
  • Grooming. A haircut that genuinely suits your face does more in an afternoon than weeks of effort elsewhere. Keep facial hair clean and intentional, whether that is a tidy beard or a clean shave. Trim your nails. Look after your teeth. These are small, unglamorous, and they read on you instantly.
  • Fitness. You do not need a particular physique. You need a body that moves well and looks cared for. Resistance training a few times a week, plus regular walking, will change your posture, your skin, your sleep, and in time your face. Eat enough protein and enough vegetables. The compounding here is slow, quiet, and real.
  • Posture. The fastest visible change on this list, and the cheapest. Most people carry themselves smaller than they are. Stand tall. Roll the shoulders back and down. Bring the chin to level. It costs nothing, and it changes how a room reads you before you have said a word.
  • Style. Clothes that fit beat clothes that are expensive. Learn your actual measurements. Choose a small palette that works on you and wear it well. Fit first, then fabric, then everything else.

If you do only these five, consistently, you will have done more than the vast majority of people who spend a year reading about it.

The quiet multipliers

Underneath the five fundamentals sit a few habits that make all of them work better, and that most beginners ignore because they are dull.

  • Sleep. Consistent, sufficient sleep shows in your skin, your eyes, and the way you hold your face. It is the cheapest improvement available, and the easiest to neglect.
  • Hydration and food. Drink enough water. Eat largely whole foods. You do not need a perfect diet, only a reasonable one repeated often.
  • Daylight and movement. Time outdoors and a daily walk steady your energy and your sleep, which loops back into everything above.

None of this is dramatic. All of it compounds. The unglamorous habits are usually the ones doing the real work.

A word on mewing and other "techniques"

You will be told that mewing, resting the tongue against the roof of the mouth, will reshape your jaw. Be careful here. There is no strong scientific evidence that mewing alters the bone structure of an adult face. It may encourage better tongue posture and nasal breathing, which are reasonable things to want, but it is not a jawline machine, and the dramatic claims around it are not supported by good evidence.

The same caution applies to most gadgets and "looksmaxxing hacks." When a single trick promises a new face, treat the promise itself as the product being sold, and walk on. Spend your attention on the fundamentals above. They are unglamorous precisely because they work.

Realistic timelines

This is where most people quit, so be honest with yourself at the start.

Some things change in days. Posture, grooming, a better haircut, clothes that fit. Address those and you will look better this week.

Most things change across seasons. Skin responds over weeks to months. Body composition, and the way training slowly reshapes a face, takes months of consistency. Hair keeps its own patient schedule.

There is no thirty-day transformation that is also permanent. The people who look dramatically different in a month either started from real neglect, where the early gains are large, or they are showing you lighting and angles. Aim for a visible difference in a season and a substantial one in a year. That is not a slow promise. It is the only honest one.

What to avoid

A short list, because the mistakes are predictable.

  • Buying before building. Routines produce results, not products. A cabinet of serums will not outperform sunscreen used every day.
  • Chasing someone else's face. Comparison is the quickest way to abandon a plan that was working. Maxx your own range.
  • Reaching for surgery out of impatience. Give softmaxxing a real season first, and involve a qualified professional for anything medical.
  • Trusting single-trick promises. Mewing claims, gadgets, extreme diets, and "one weird routine" pitches are noise dressed as shortcuts.
  • Measuring daily. Change at this pace is invisible day to day and obvious month to month. Photograph yourself monthly, in the same light, and compare then.
  • Letting it curdle into self-punishment. The aim is to look like the rested, cared-for, best version of yourself. Not to wage a war on the face you already have.

A simple way to begin

If you want one starting move, make it this: find out where you actually stand, then change one thing at a time. Knowing your own baseline removes the guesswork and quiets the comparison. It tells you which of the five fundamentals will move your presence the most, so your effort lands in one place instead of spreading thin across all of them at once.
